    Why do transition metals form alloys?

    Because transition metals have similar atomic sizes (radii), atoms of one metal can easily slide into and occupy positions in the crystal lattice of another metal. This produces extremely strong alloys with high melting points and corrosion resistance.

    What is Lanthanoid Contraction?

    As atomic number increases from 57 to 71, the nuclear charge increases by +1 at each step, and electrons are added to the 4f subshell.

    Crucial Fact: The shielding effect of 4f electrons is extremely poor due to their highly diffused shapes.

    As a result, the outer 6s electrons experience a stronger effective nuclear charge, pulling them closer to the nucleus. This causes the atomic and ionic sizes to contract steadily.

    Consequence: Chemical Twins

    Due to Lanthanoid contraction, elements of the 4d series (Period 5) and 5d series (Period 6) have almost identical atomic radii. Let's compare Zr (Z=40) and Hf (Z=72):

    Zr (Z=40) Radius 160 pm
    Hf (Z=72) Radius 159 pm

    Despite Hafnium having an extra shell, they have identical sizes! This makes them extremely difficult to separate chemically.
